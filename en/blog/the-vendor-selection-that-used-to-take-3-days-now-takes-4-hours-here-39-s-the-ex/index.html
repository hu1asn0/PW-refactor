The Vendor Selection That Used to Take 3 Days Now Takes 4 Hours — Here&#39;s the Exact AI Workflow

András Simon

🎯 INTRODUCTION

Dear Project Management Colleagues,

You know the situation. Three vendors, three RFP responses at 80–120 pages each, a decision deadline in 10 days — and the rest of the project isn't pausing while you work through it. You build a scoring spreadsheet, start reading the proposals, and somewhere around hour six you realize you're still on vendor one, and the page you're on is the appendix.

Vendor selection is one of the most time-intensive tasks a PM faces, and one of the most consequential. You'll live with this decision for two to five years. Miss a vendor lock-in clause in the contract, misread the SLA exceptions, fail to check whether the preferred vendor's key delivery team is the same one that's been quietly walking out the door — and the project cost goes up long after the signing.

The full manual process — background research, RFP synthesis, comparison matrix, red flag review, executive summary — realistically takes 17–28 person-hours across 3–4 working days. That's time most PMs don't have in clean, uninterrupted blocks.

In this issue, I'll walk you through a complete AI-assisted vendor selection workflow. Perplexity Deep Research for vendor due diligence. Perplexity for RFP synthesis, scoring matrix generation, and contract red flag identification. And a final executive summary prompt that turns the analysis into a decision-ready steering committee presentation. Every prompt is copy-paste ready.

📊 CONTEXT — WHY THIS MATTERS NOW

The vendor landscape has become harder to navigate, not easier. The B2B software market exceeded 30,000 products by 2024. Implementation partner ecosystems have doubled in size in five years. And vendor contracts have grown longer and more complex — with more exceptions, liability caps, data portability restrictions, and auto-renewal traps buried in the fine print.

According to Gartner's 2025 procurement technology report, 60% of enterprise procurement teams plan to adopt AI tools in vendor evaluation workflows by 2026. McKinsey's 2024 analysis of AI in due diligence found a 40–60% reduction in evaluation cycle time for teams using structured AI analysis — with a measurable reduction in "hidden risk" contract surprises.

🔍 PRACTICAL EXAMPLE: STEP BY STEP

The Scenario: ERP Implementation Partner Selection, 250-Person Manufacturing Company

Situation: You are the IT project lead at a 250-person manufacturing company. The company is implementing SAP S/4HANA and has received RFP responses from three implementation partners. Each response is 80–100 pages, each follows a different structure, each uses different terminology for the same concepts. Decision deadline: 10 days. Meanwhile, a parallel project is in flight.

The challenges:

  • Three proposals, three different structures — comparing them directly is comparing apples to oranges

  • One vendor has an informal reputation for delivery issues, but there's no time to investigate properly

  • The contract draft from the preferred vendor contains several clauses that feel non-standard, and legal review is three days out

Step 1: Vendor background research — Perplexity Deep Research

Before you read a single page of any RFP, understand who you're dealing with. Use Perplexity in Deep Research mode — not a standard search, but a 10–15 minute multi-source synthesis that aggregates real-time information across company databases, review platforms, news sources, and analyst reports.

Open Perplexity, select Deep Research mode, and run this prompt for each vendor separately:

Conduct a comprehensive due diligence summary on the following company,
which is being evaluated as a SAP S/4HANA implementation partner:

Company name: [Vendor name]
Approximate size (if known): [employee count]
Services: SAP ERP implementation, [country/region]

Research and synthesize across the following dimensions:

1. FINANCIAL STABILITY
   - Revenue trend over the past 2–3 years (if available)
   - Ownership structure, recent acquisitions or divestitures
   - Any signs of financial distress, payment issues, or credit risk

2. REPUTATION AND REFERENCES
   - Known client references (industry, company size)
   - Ratings on G2, Gartner Peer Insights, Trustpilot
   - Negative press coverage, litigation, compliance incidents

3. MARKET POSITION
   - SAP partner tier (Gold, Platinum, etc.)
   - Industry and vertical specializations
   - Competitive positioning: where strong, where weak

4. RISK SIGNALS
   - Key personnel changes or high turnover signals
   - Capacity questions (are they overextended?)
   - Strength of regional presence for this engagement

Output: Structured summary, 400–500 words, with bullet points
per dimension. Flag anything you could not independently verify.

What you get back: In 10–15 minutes, an aggregated vendor profile covering financials, client reputation, market positioning, and risk signals — with source citations.

Time: 15–20 minutes per vendor (run in parallel)

Step 2: RFP synthesis — Perplexity

Upload the RFP PDFs to Perplexity (Claude 3.5 Sonnet or Opus handles long documents well) and run this prompt for each response:

The attached document is an RFP response from a SAP S/4HANA
implementation partner. Read it and produce a structured summary.

[ATTACHED: RFP document PDF]

The summary should cover:

1. PROJECT APPROACH
   - Implementation methodology (waterfall / agile / hybrid)
   - Proposed project phases and milestones
   - Estimated project duration

2. TEAM AND CAPACITY
   - Proposed team composition (roles, FTE headcount)
   - Key personnel experience (if named)
   - Use of subcontractors (yes / no / unclear)

3. PRICING
   - Total bid price (if stated)
   - Pricing model (fixed price / T&M / hybrid)
   - What is explicitly NOT included in the price?

4. RISK MANAGEMENT
   - How do they handle scope changes?
   - Warranty and support terms
   - Hypercare period and transition support

5. EDITORIAL NOTES
   - The single strongest element of this proposal
   - The area least developed or most vague

Output format: Tables where possible, maximum 600 words total.
Flag ambiguities — do not infer what is not stated.

What you get back: Three summaries in identical structure — comparison becomes straightforward.

Time: 10–15 minutes per document

Step 3: Generate the comparison scoring matrix

With the three summaries, ask Perplexity to build the evaluation matrix:

Below are structured summaries of three SAP implementation partner
RFP responses (all in identical format):

VENDOR A summary:
[Step 2 output — Vendor A]

VENDOR B summary:
[Step 2 output — Vendor B]

VENDOR C summary:
[Step 2 output — Vendor C]

Evaluation criteria and weights (based on our project priorities):
- Methodology maturity: 25%
- Team experience and stability: 25%
- Pricing (value for cost): 20%
- Risk management approach: 15%
- References (from Step 1 Perplexity research): 15%

Please:
1. Score each vendor on each criterion on a 1–5 scale
   with a one-sentence justification
2. Calculate the weighted total score for each vendor
3. Write a 3–4 sentence decision recommendation:
   which vendor is lowest risk, which offers best price,
   which is strongest methodologically
4. Flag dimensions where data was incomplete or ambiguous

Output: Markdown table + decision recommendation text.

What you get back: A weighted scoring matrix with justified scores and a decision narrative — formatted for a steering committee presentation.

Time: 5–8 minutes

Step 4: Contract red flag identification

Once you have the contract draft (or if the RFP includes contract terms), run a red flag scan before legal review:

Read the following IT vendor contract sections and identify
risk factors from a project management perspective — not as
a lawyer, but as an experienced IT PM who knows the common
patterns of vendor lock-in and risk-shifting in technology contracts.

[PASTE THE CONTRACT SECTIONS: SLA, LIABILITY, TERMINATION, PRICING]

Search across the following risk categories:

GREEN flags (standard, acceptable market terms):
- Bilateral termination rights
- Liability cap at 2–3x annual contract value
- Guaranteed data portability upon termination

YELLOW flags (negotiable, requires attention):
- Short warranty period (< 90 days)
- Ambiguous scope change governance
- Long list of SLA exceptions

RED flags (high risk, should be challenged or renegotiated):
- Auto-renewal with 60+ day cancellation requirement
- Unilateral right to change pricing
- Uncapped liability on the client side
- No data portability or exit assistance obligation

For each identified flag:
- Quote the relevant contract language
- Risk level: HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW
- Suggested negotiation position (what to ask for instead)

Output: Prioritized risk list with negotiation recommendations.

What you get back: A PM-perspective risk scan — not legal advice, but a clear map of the highest-priority items to bring into the negotiation before legal review.

Time: 15–20 minutes per contract

Step 5: Executive summary for the steering committee

Combine all inputs into a decision-ready presentation brief:

Based on the following analysis materials (vendor due diligence,
RFP comparison, scoring matrix, red flag review):

Perplexity background research summaries:
[Step 1 outputs]

Scoring matrix final results:
[Step 3 output]

Key red flags by vendor:
[Step 4 output]

Write an executive summary for the steering committee covering:

1. RECOMMENDED DECISION (1–2 sentences): which vendor to select and why
2. DECISION CRITERIA (table): top 5 evaluation dimensions compared
3. RISKS (bullet list): key risks with the recommended vendor
   and proposed mitigations
4. CONDITIONS (if any): what contractual or process conditions
   should the decision be contingent on
5. NEXT STEPS: 3–5 concrete action items following the decision

Style: Concise and decision-oriented, max 400 words.
The steering committee needs a clear recommendation with
rationale — not a detailed analysis recap.

What you get back: A complete steering committee brief, ready to present — without spending three days writing it.

Time: 5–8 minutes

📚 COOKBOOK — PRACTICAL RECIPES

Recipe #1: Rapid vendor background check — "Who is this company, really?"

The problem: You're evaluating an unfamiliar vendor. You need to quickly understand their financial stability, client reputation, and whether there's any recent bad news — before the first meeting.

Prompt:

Run a rapid due diligence research on the following vendor
using Perplexity Deep Research mode:

Company name: [Vendor name]
Industry/service: [IT services, SaaS, consulting, etc.]
Country of operations: [Country]

Answer the following:
1. When were they founded, who owns them, and have they been
   acquired or restructured in the last 3 years?
2. What is their annual revenue (if publicly available)?
3. What are their known clients, and what company sizes
   do they typically serve?
4. Any negative news in the last 12 months?
   (lawsuits, data breaches, layoffs, executive departures)
5. What do their Gartner Peer Insights or G2 reviews say?
   Recurring positives and negatives?

Output: Structured summary, max 300 words, with source links.

How fast it works: 10–15 minutes

Result: A reliable vendor profile aggregating all publicly available signals — before you sit down at the negotiating table

Recipe #2: Two-RFP quick comparison — "Cut through the noise"

The problem: Two proposals arrived in completely different formats and you can't directly compare the critical terms without spending hours normalizing them.

Prompt:

Compare the following two vendor proposals for an IT implementation
project.

PROPOSAL 1: [Vendor A name]
[Paste key sections: price, timeline, methodology, team, warranty]

PROPOSAL 2: [Vendor B name]
[Paste key sections: same structure]

Compare on:
- Project duration and milestone structure
- Team size and key roles
- Pricing model and what is explicitly excluded
- Risk management approach
- Support and hypercare terms post go-live

Output: Side-by-side comparison table + 3-sentence assessment
of which proposal is more transparent and why.

How fast it works: 5–8 minutes

Result: A normalized comparison that surfaces gaps and omissions in either proposal — not just the differences

Recipe #3: SLA and contract red flag scan

The problem: You have the contract draft, but legal review is three days away. You want to walk into that review knowing what to look for.

Prompt:

Review the following IT service contract sections and identify
the most critical risk factors from a project delivery perspective.

[PASTE: SLA, Liability, Termination, and Pricing sections]

Flag issues in these categories:

VENDOR LOCK-IN
- Data portability and migration restrictions
- Long notice periods, auto-renewal terms
- Proprietary formats or API exclusivity

FINANCIAL RISK
- Unilateral price change rights
- Uncapped cost overrun exposure
- Hidden fees or penalty structures

SLA LOOPHOLES
- Length and breadth of SLA exclusions list
- Liability cap amount (too low?)
- Incident response time commitments vs. reality

CONTRACTUAL IMBALANCE
- Who has final say on scope changes?
- Termination for convenience terms
- IP ownership for deliverables

For each flag: quote the contract language, assign risk level
(HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW), and give a one-sentence negotiation ask.

How fast it works: 15–20 minutes

Result: A prioritized risk map you can bring into legal review — you arrive knowing what matters, not discovering it after the fact

Recipe #4: Weighted decision matrix from stakeholder inputs

The problem: Different steering committee members weight criteria differently. One prioritizes cost, one prioritizes references, one prioritizes risk. You need a defensible, objective aggregate score.

Prompt:

Build a weighted decision matrix for the following vendor selection.

EVALUATION CRITERIA AND WEIGHTS (from stakeholder alignment):
- Technical approach: 20%
- Team experience: 25%
- Total cost of ownership: 20%
- References: 20%
- Risk management: 15%

VENDOR SCORES (1–5 scale, from RFP analysis):

Vendor A:
- Technical approach: [score] — [justification]
- Team experience: [score] — [justification]
- TCO: [score] — [justification]
- References: [score] — [justification]
- Risk management: [score] — [justification]

Vendor B: [same format]
Vendor C: [same format]

Calculate weighted totals. Provide a 3–4 sentence decision
recommendation. Flag any dimension where the data was incomplete
or the score is uncertain.

Output: Table + written recommendation.

How fast it works: 5 minutes

Result: A documented, defensible decision matrix you can present and defend to any stakeholder — built in minutes, not hours

💡 CLOSING THOUGHT

Vendor selection is not a process optimization exercise — it is a strategic decision you will live with for years. AI does not make the decision for you. It does not replace reference calls, negotiation experience, or legal review.

What it does is give you something most PMs can't manufacture under tight deadlines: genuine comparability. When all three proposals are synthesized in the same structure, when risks are measured on the same scale, and when vendor backgrounds are surfaced from public sources before the first meeting — the quality of the decision improves significantly.

Run the Step 4 red flag prompt on your next contract draft before legal review. The negotiation that follows will be more structured because you already know what's in there.
