Work Smart, Not Numb: Using Perplexity to Keep Energy for Your Life After Work

András Simon

As an IT project manager, if you get home every night feeling like a drained battery, it’s usually not about you – it’s about the system you’re operating in. Constant context switching, endless status pings, manual reporting, and scattered information eat exactly the energy you’d like to spend on your partner, family, dog, or simply yourself.

Perplexity can become your daily cockpit assistant: you outsource mental sorting, prioritisation, and summarisation, so you can save your limited willpower for what really matters.

Step-by-step example – A 15-minute “PM cockpit” with Perplexity

Goal: A short morning routine that structures your workday so your brain can actually switch off in the evening.

Step 1 – Gather your daily inputs

From your calendar and task system, pull together:

  • Today’s meetings (time, participants, topic)

  • Top 10 open tasks (tickets, emails, decisions)

  • One sentence on how much energy you have today

It doesn’t have to be perfect; it just has to reflect reality.

Step 2 – Ask Perplexity for a focused plan

Use a prompt like:

„Here are my meetings and tasks for today, plus my current energy level.
- Highlight 3 key outcomes I can realistically achieve today.
- Suggest time blocks for deep work.
- Create a short, prioritised to-do list (max 8 items) I can follow.”

Paste your list below this prompt.

Step 3 – Block your deep work

From the answer:

  • Select the 2–3 most important outcomes (your “big rocks”).

  • Block the suggested deep-work times into your calendar (e.g. 9:30–11:00 and 14:00–15:00).

The aim is that you don’t spend the day only reacting. You have at least two windows where you drive the agenda, not your inbox.

Step 4 – Filter out noise

Then ask:

„From this task list, identify which items could be delegated, postponed, or dropped, and recommend what to do with each.”

You’ll get a quick lens on what you don’t need to carry personally today.

Step 5 – Mini shutdown at the end of the day

Before you leave (or close your laptop at home):

„Summarise what I achieved today in 5 bullet points, and write a 5–7 item focus list for tomorrow, with no more than 3 big rocks.”

Save that output somewhere you’ll open tomorrow morning. This becomes your mental handover from “today you” to “tomorrow you”, and it lets your brain stop spinning in the evening.

Cookbook – Surviving meeting overload: weekly “meeting compression” with Perplexity

Recipe name: „Meeting Compression – Turning 3 Hours of Calls Into 1.5 Hours of Real Focus”

Ingredients

  • Next week’s meeting calendar (Outlook/Google/Teams export or screenshot list)

  • Any existing meeting notes or short purpose descriptions

  • 30–45 minutes of quiet time with Perplexity

Preparation

1. Build a meeting inventory

List for the coming week:

  • Meeting title and purpose (status / decision / workshop / 1:1 / other)

  • Who attends

  • Duration

You now see your week as blocks of attention, not just coloured rectangles.

2. Classify by value

Prompt:

„Based on the meeting list below, categorise each meeting by value:

- High value: Without me, the project or decision would not move.
- Medium value: Useful, but could partially be replaced by async updates.
- Low value: Mainly information sharing that could be consumed via a written summary.

Also suggest which meetings could be shortened, made less frequent, or replaced by async status updates.”

Paste your inventory.

3. Generate async alternatives

For the low-value set, ask Perplexity:

„Create a status update template that could replace this type of meeting. 
Include: context, current status, key risks/blockers, decisions needed.”

You can adapt this into a recurring written status mail or channel post.

4. Draft your personal “meeting policy”

Prompt:

„Write a 1-page ‘Meeting Policy for IT PMs’ that explains:
Which types of meetings I keep as live sessions;
Which I convert to async updates;
What criteria must be met before a meeting gets into my calendar at all.
Tone: professional but practical.”

This becomes your internal guideline (and, where appropriate, a gentle boundary-setting tool with colleagues).

Outcome

  • 1–2 recurring meetings can often be turned into async updates.

  • You reduce context switching and reclaim deep-work time.

  • By the time you’re heading home, you’re less mentally shredded – with more energy left for your partner, family, dog, and yourself.
