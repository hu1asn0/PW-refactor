How to Build Your Mental Check-Out Protocol

András Simon

Goal: A personalized, repeatable shutdown ritual that helps you mentally leave work behind before the holiday break.

  1. Audit your open loops.
    Before you can mentally close work, you need to know what's still open. Ask Perplexity:

I'm an IT project manager wrapping up the year. Help me create a structured "open loops audit" checklist — covering project status, team handoffs, stakeholder communications, and personal commitments — so nothing falls through the cracks before a 2-week holiday break.

You'll get a ready-to-use checklist. The act of writing it all down is itself a cognitive relief — your brain stops holding it all in the background.​

  1. Write an OOO message that actually sets a boundary.
    Generic OOO messages don't protect you. This one will:

Write a professional out-of-office email for an IT project manager going on a 2-week Christmas holiday. Clearly communicate who handles what, set expectations that I will NOT check email, and sound warm but firm. Audience: internal team and external stakeholders.

Use it verbatim or tweak it — but commit to it fully, and communicate it to your team.​

  1. Design your personal shutdown ritual.
    A ritual signals to your brain: work is over. Ask Perplexity:

Suggest a 15-minute end-of-workday shutdown ritual for a senior IT project manager who struggles to mentally disconnect from work during the holidays. Include physical, digital, and cognitive steps.

Pick 3–4 steps that feel realistic to you. Write them on a Post-it. Do them every single day of the holiday.tranquiljc+1

  1. Create a "worry parking lot."
    Your brain will generate work thoughts during the holiday — don't fight it, park them:

Create a simple "worry parking lot" template for a project manager during holiday break. It should let me capture a work thought in under 20 seconds, assign it a priority level, and park it for January — so I stop ruminating and return to the present moment.

Keep this in a notes app. Each time a work thought intrudes, park it and return to your family.​

  1. Write a presence commitment — and read it on December 23rd.
    Ask Perplexity:

Help me write a short, honest personal commitment statement (5–7 sentences) about being mentally present with my family during Christmas. I'm an IT project manager who tends to overthink work. Make it grounding, not cheesy.

On the evening of December 23rd — just before you close the laptop for the last time — read it out loud to yourself. It sounds small, but articulating it shifts behavior.​

  1. Prepare your re-entry plan before you leave.
    Knowing you have a structured return plan actually reduces your holiday anxiety. Ask:

I'm an IT project manager going on a 2-week holiday break. Help me write a quick "re-entry checklist" to get back up to speed in January without overwhelming myself on day one.

Write it, save it, and then let it go. You've trusted the plan. Now trust the holiday​

💡 Tip: Perplexity responds best to English prompts — but you can always add "Please respond in French" at the end of any prompt.

The Pre-Holiday Comms Sweep

This recipe is for those whose holiday anxiety isn't about their own thoughts — it's about open stakeholder expectations.

Ingredients:

  • Your top 5–8 project stakeholders

  • Current project status for each (rough notes are fine)

  • 30 minutes, before December 20th

  • Perplexity open in a browser tab

Preparation:

  1. Generate your status update template:

Write a concise, professional pre-holiday project status update email template for IT project stakeholders. Cover: current status (RAG), key accomplishments this quarter, what's paused over the break, who to contact for emergencies, and next steps in January. Tone: confident and reassuring.

  1. Adapt for your most anxious stakeholder:

Adapt this status update email for a business stakeholder who is non-technical and tends to worry about project delays. Keep it under 150 words, focus on business value delivered, and end on a confident, reassuring note.

Repeat for 2–3 stakeholder types (technical lead, executive sponsor, external vendor).​

  1. Write your team's year-end thank-you:

Write a warm but professional end-of-year message from an IT project manager to their team. Acknowledge the hard work, reference 2–3 themes without fake specifics, set clear expectations for the break, and close with genuine energy for the new year. Max 200 words.

  1. Schedule everything for December 20th at 10 AM. Done.

Outcome:
By December 20th, every stakeholder has a clear, reassuring update and your team feels genuinely appreciated. Your inbox anxiety drops noticeably — because you've proactively closed every open communication loop. You arrive at Christmas Eve with a quieter mind.aplin+1
