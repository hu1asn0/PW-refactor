Perplexity at Year-End: Close Your Projects Like a Pro, Host Christmas Like a Boss

András Simon

1 brain, 2 worlds (here’s 3 brains anyways 🧠🧠🧠)

Year-end for an IT project manager means tight deadlines, KPI reports, budget reviews – while at the same time planning Christmas dinner, managing guests, and trying not to burn out. Perplexity﻿ can be the same assistant for both: helping you close your project year professionally and design a stress-free holiday at home.

1. Professional life – Year-end project closure with Perplexity 📆

1.1. Step-by-step example – Year-end project portfolio summary

Scenario:
Multiple IT projects closed during the year, and you must deliver a concise, executive-friendly year-end summary: status, KPIs, budget, lessons learned.

Step 1 – Dump the data, then let AI structure it

  • Export basic project data (e.g., project name, status, planned vs. actual budget, timeline, major issues, KPIs).

  • Write a prompt explaining these are IT projects and you need a year-end executive summary with clear bullets and lessons.

Example prompt:

„Please create a 1-page year-end summary of the following IT projects. Highlight:﻿

  • KPI performance (time, cost, scope, quality);﻿

  • key success factors;﻿

  • lessons learned that could be turned into governance rules next year.”﻿

Step 2 – KPI-centric analysis

Ask Perplexity to:

  • Group projects in a table by KPIs (on-time, budget variance %, scope change level, number of major incidents).

  • Flag the top 3﻿ best-performing and top 3﻿ riskiest projects, with short justification.

Step 3 – Generate a presentation skeleton

  • „Draft a 6–8 slide outline for a senior leadership presentation:

  • 1) overall view of the year,

  • 2) key wins,

  • 3) major challenges,

  • 4) lessons learned,

  • 5) recommendations for next year.”﻿

  • Move this outline into your slide deck tool, add visuals and branding.

Outcome:
What normally takes 1–2 days of manual reporting and Excel grinding can be turned into a 1–2 hour, structured, AI-assisted workflow.

1.2. Cookbook – AI-assisted success factors & lessons learned 👨‍🏫

Recipe name: “Lessons Learned 2.0 – AI-powered yearly retrospective for IT projects”﻿

Ingredients:

  • 3–5 retrospective docs or meeting notes from closed projects

  • Core KPIs (time, cost, scope, quality)

  • 30–45 focused minutes

  • Perplexity﻿ with well-crafted prompts

Preparation:

  1. Feed project context

    • „Based on the following project retrospective notes, identify common success factors and recurring issues. Group them by theme (communication, scope, technical debt, stakeholder alignment, etc.).”﻿

  2. Turn success factors into “rules”

    • „Formulate 5–7 concrete ‘golden rules’ we should integrate into our project governance next year. Add a short explanation and an example for each.”﻿

  3. Produce leadership-level summary

    • „Write a max 1-page ‘Lessons Learned – 2025 IT Projects’ executive summary with 3 concrete recommendations for next year.”﻿

  4. Reuse for next year

    • Integrate the golden rules and summary into your kickoff workshop or PM onboarding material.

2. Personal life – Holiday menu planning with Perplexity

2.1. Step-by-step: Christmas menu with dietary constraints and a strict deadline! 🌲🎄🎁

Scenario:
Family Christmas for 8–10 people: some are gluten-free, some vegans, others have allergies. You want a coherent menu that feels festive, yet works for everyone.

Step 1 – Define the menu frame

  • „Please propose a 4-course Christmas menu for 8 people that is:﻿

    • gluten-free;﻿

    • includes at least 2 vegan dishes;﻿

    • avoids the following allergens: …;﻿

    • inspired by Central European / Hungarian flavours with a modern twist.”﻿

Step 2 – Recipes, ingredients, shopping list

  • Ask for each dish: short description, prep time, key ingredients.

  • Then: „Generate one consolidated shopping list with quantities for 8 people.”﻿

Step 3 – Wine and drinks pairing

  • „Provide wine and drink pairing suggestions for each course, including at least one alcohol-free option per dish.”﻿

Step 4 – Communicating with guests

  • „Draft a short, friendly message to my family, explaining that the menu is designed to respect dietary needs and summarizing what we’ll eat.”﻿

2.2. Cookbook – Stress-free holiday meal prep with a PM mindset 👩‍🍳

Recipe name: “Christmas as a project – Holiday meal prep with Perplexity instead of a Gantt chart”﻿

Ingredients:

  • 2–3 days before the celebration

  • Final list of dishes and recipes

  • Knowledge of your kitchen capacity (oven, stove, fridge)

  • A PM’s structured way of thinking

Preparation:

  1. Time-based breakdown

    • „Break down the preparation of this menu into tasks I can do:﻿

      • 2 days before;﻿

      • 1 day before;﻿

      • on the morning of the dinner;﻿

      • 1–2 hours before guests arrive.”﻿

  2. Parallelisation and priorities

    • „Create a simple timeline showing which tasks I can run in parallel to minimise chaos in the kitchen.”﻿

  3. Risk management in the kitchen

    • „List 5 typical risks (e.g. something burns, dessert is late, oven capacity issues) and suggest contingency plans for each.”﻿

  4. Involving kids and guests

    • „Suggest tasks that are safe and simple for kids or guests to help with, so they support instead of disturb.”﻿

Outcome:
You get a mini “meal prep project plan” that you can follow like a sprint board – but the deliverable is a relaxed, enjoyable holiday dinner.
