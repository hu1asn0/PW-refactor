Wake Up Looking Forward to the Day, Not Afraid of It

András Simon

When your brain never clocks out

As an IT project manager, your day rarely stops when the last meeting ends. Your mind keeps replaying unfinished tasks, tricky stakeholders, risks, and “what ifs” long after you’ve closed your laptop. Falling asleep is hard; staying asleep is harder.

Motivation in the morning is almost impossible if your brain never got a real shutdown. The key is to stop trusting your memory and mental spinning, and instead offload your thoughts into a trusted system that will be there in the morning. Perplexity can be that system: an external brain that holds your worries and turns them into structured, realistic plans you can wake up to.

Step-by-step example – Nightly brain dump + 10-minute morning briefing

Goal:
Clear your head before sleep, and start your day with a short, focused briefing instead of a wave of anxiety.

You’ll create a simple two-part ritual:

  1. A nightly brain dump where you push everything out of your head into structured notes.

  2. A morning briefing where Perplexity turns those notes into a realistic plan for the day.

Step 1 – Do a raw nightly brain dump (10–15 minutes)

At the end of your workday or before bed:

  • Open a note titled something like [Daily Brain Dump – YYYY-MM-DD].

  • Without editing yourself, list everything that’s on your mind:

    • Work tasks you didn’t finish.

    • Emails you still “should” answer.

    • Risks or issues you’re worried about.

    • Personal to-dos (family, health, errands, finances).

    • Any “don’t forget tomorrow…” thoughts.

The idea is to get it out of your head and onto the page, not to make it pretty.

Step 2 – Ask Perplexity to structure and prioritise it

Copy your entire brain dump into a new chat and use a prompt like this:

Here is a raw end-of-day brain dump from me as an IT project manager.  Please: 
1) Turn it into a structured to-do list with two sections: WORK and LIFE. 
2) Mark urgent items with a * and items that can be deferred to next week with a ~. 
3) Create a "Big Rocks for Tomorrow" list with 3–5 items that should be my main focus.  
Keep it concise and practical, not motivational fluff.  [BRAIN DUMP PASTED HERE]

Perplexity will:

  • Separate work and life.

  • Indicate what’s urgent vs. deferrable.

  • Extract a focused “Big Rocks for Tomorrow” list.

At this point, your mind can stop looping, because there is a written, structured record.

Step 3 – Create a “parking lot” for everything that’s not for tomorrow

From the answer:

  • Take all the items marked with ~ and paste them into a separate section or note called Parking / Next Week / Later.

  • Leave them there intentionally. They are captured, so your brain doesn’t need to keep them active.

Mentally, you’re saying: “This is important, but not now. It’s safe in the system.”

Step 4 – Sleep on it – your brain can rest

Before you go to bed, briefly skim:

  • The Big Rocks for Tomorrow list.

  • The fact that everything else is safely captured.

You do not need to plan further. The point is to tell your brain: “You can stop reminding me – it’s all written down and I’ll have help turning it into a plan in the morning.”

Step 5 – Morning: 10-minute AI-powered briefing

The next morning, open last night’s Perplexity answer and run a second prompt on top of it, using the Big Rocks and to-do structure.

Prompt suggestion:

Yesterday you helped me turn my brain dump into a structured list.  Now please: 
1) Remind me of the "Big Rocks for Tomorrow" you identified. 
2) Create a realistic "Day Plan" for today, assuming I only have 2–3 deep work blocks    and I will get interrupted by ad hoc requests. 
3) Keep the plan to one screen: clear bullet points I can actually follow.  
[TASK SUMMARY / BIG ROCKS PASTED HERE, IF NEEDED]

You now have:

  • 3–5 key outcomes (not just tasks).

  • A simple flow for your day (e.g. morning deep work, midday admin, afternoon decisions).

  • A plan that already accounts for reality (interruptions, limited energy).

Step 6 – Adjust for your actual energy level

Some mornings you wake up at 100%, some at 40%. A static plan doesn’t care; Perplexity can.

Add a quick follow-up:

Assume that today I am only at about 70% energy.  Given the Day Plan you just created: 
1) Which items would you drop completely for today? 
2) Which items would you delegate or simplify? 
3) If I could only do 3 things today, which 3 would you choose and why?  
Keep it short and decisive.

Now your plan fits the version of you that actually woke up, not an idealised robot version.

Step 7 – Keep the briefing visible

  • Copy the final Day Plan into your notes, task app, or even onto a sticky note.

  • Keep it open or in front of you as a one-page brief for the day.

Whenever you feel overwhelmed or lost, look at it and ask: “What was the next agreed thing?” – and go back to that.

Cookbook – 10‑minute morning “standup with yourself” using Perplexity

Recipe name: “Morning Mini-Standup With Yourself – Powered by Perplexity”﻿

The idea: treat your morning like a short daily standup where you are both the PM and the team. Perplexity plays the role of facilitator and scribe.

Ingredients

  • Calendar for the day (meetings, key time blocks).

  • List of your main open tasks (or a rough memory of them).

  • A rough sense of your current energy level (1–10).

  • 10 minutes and a cup of coffee or tea.

Step 1 – Share your raw inputs

Prepare a short text including:

  • Today’s meetings (time + short purpose).

  • 5–10 important open tasks.

  • Your current energy level (e.g. “I feel like a 6/10 today”).

  • One sentence on how you want to show up today (e.g. “calm”, “decisive”, “supportive”, “focused”).

Step 2 – Ask for a structured Daily Briefing

Prompt template:

Act as my personal chief of staff for today.  
Here is my context: 
- Meetings: [list] 
- Key open tasks: [list] 
- Current energy level (1–10): [your number] 
- How I want to show up today: [e.g. calm, decisive, supportive]  Please create a concise "Daily Briefing" for me that includes: 
1) 3 primary outcomes I should aim to achieve today (not just tasks). 
2) Suggested deep work blocks in my schedule, if any are available.
3) A prioritised to-do list with no more than 8 items, aligned with the outcomes. 
4) 3 specific behavioural reminders that will help me show up the way I want (e.g. "pause before responding", "ask one clarifying question", etc.).  
Keep it to one screen and use clear bullet points.

This gives you:

  • Clear outcomes (what success looks like today).

  • A realistic sequence of work.

  • Behavioural cues that align with your intention for the day.

Step 3 – Prepare a “minimal success” fallback plan

Life happens. Some days derail. You can pre‑plan how to cope.

Run this follow-up:

Using the Daily Briefing you just created:  If today went sideways (emergencies, fire-fighting, unexpected issues) and I could only do the bare minimum, answer:  
1) What are the 3 bare-minimum actions I should still try to complete    to call today a "win"? 
2) Which parts of the plan can be safely dropped or postponed to tomorrow?  
Keep it very concrete and short.

You now have two levels of success:

  • Full plan – if the day goes reasonably as expected.

  • Minimal win – if everything blows up, but you still want to feel you moved the ball.

Step 4 – Turn it into a repeatable ritual

You can save both prompts (Daily Briefing + Minimal Success) as text snippets or templates in your notes. Each morning:

  1. Paste your updated context (meetings, tasks, energy, intention).

  2. Run the Daily Briefing prompt.

  3. Run the Minimal Success prompt.

  4. Copy the outputs into your daily note.

After a few days, this 10‑minute flow starts to:

  • Lower the “dread factor” when you wake up.

  • Replace vague anxiety with a concrete, aligned plan.

  • Make it easier to feel good about your day even if it wasn’t perfect.
