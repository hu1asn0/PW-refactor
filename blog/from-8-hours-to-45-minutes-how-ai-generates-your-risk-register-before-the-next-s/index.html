From 8 Hours to 45 Minutes: How AI Generates Your Risk Register Before the Next Steering Committee

András Simon

🎯 INTRODUCTION

Dear Project Management Colleagues,

There is a task that most project managers quietly avoid. It sits at the bottom of the to-do list, week after week. Its name: risk register update. You know it needs to be done. The steering committee knows it needs to be done. But manually building a thorough risk register for an ERP rollout or cloud migration easily consumes 4–8 working hours — and that time rarely materializes.

What if you could do it in 45 minutes?

Not with shortcuts that compromise quality — but with a well-crafted Perplexity prompt and the right workflow. In this issue, I'll walk you through how to generate a complete, editable risk register for an SAP S/4HANA implementation project, step by step. Every prompt is copy-paste ready. At the end, a cookbook section helps you adapt these patterns to your own project context.

📊 CONTEXT — WHY THIS MATTERS NOW

70% of IT projects run late or over budget. Risk management quality is consistently cited as one of the top differentiators between successful and failing projects. That's not new data. What is new: by 2025–2026, large language models have reached a level of practical usefulness where they can generate structured, context-specific risk documentation — not generic checklists, but risk registers grounded in your actual project parameters.

According to PMI's 2024 Pulse of the Profession, the average IT PM spends 3–5 hours per week on risk management documentation. The identification, categorization, probability/impact assessment, and initial mitigation drafts — all of this can now be delegated to an AI as a first-pass.

🔍 PRACTICAL EXAMPLE: STEP BY STEP

The Scenario: SAP S/4HANA Implementation, 300 Users

Situation: You are the project manager on a 1,200-person industrial company's SAP ERP implementation. Two weeks after kickoff, the steering committee requests a risk register — due next week. The full project team isn't assembled yet. There is no time for a two-day risk workshop.

The challenges:

  • No complete project team available for a structured workshop

  • Institutional knowledge from past SAP projects is fragmented across individuals

  • Steering committee is non-technical — they need a clear, executive-ready format

Step 1: Context prompt — establish the risk landscape with Perplexity

Open Perlpexiyte (perplexity.ai ). Paste this prompt and fill in your project details:

You are an experienced IT project management consultant
specializing in SAP implementations.

My project context:
- Project type: SAP S/4HANA greenfield implementation
- Company size: 1,200 employees, industrial manufacturing
- Modules in scope: FI, CO, MM, PP, SD
- Project duration: 18 months
- Team size: 35 people (internal + external consultants)
- Current phase: Blueprint / design phase (week 3)
- Critical integration: MES system (manufacturing execution)

Please identify 20–25 realistic risks for this project,
organized across these categories:
- Technical risks
- Organizational / people risks
- Project management risks
- External / vendor risks
- Security / compliance risks

For each risk, provide:
1. Risk name and description (2–3 sentences)
2. Probability: Low / Medium / High
3. Impact: Low / Medium / High / Critical
4. Risk score (Probability × Impact, scale 1–9)
5. Recommended mitigation steps (3–4 concrete actions)
6. Responsible role (e.g., PM, Technical Lead, Sponsor)

Format: Markdown table, exportable to Excel.

What you get back: 20–25 structured risk items, categorized, prioritized, with mitigation guidance — in 2–3 minutes.

Step 2: Industry benchmarks — Perplexity Deep Research

Perplexity's risks are grounded in your project context. Now augment them with industry precedents gathered by Perplexity from documented project experience.

Using Deep Research mode, find answers to the following:

1. What are the most common risks in SAP S/4HANA greenfield
   implementations at industrial manufacturing companies?
2. Which risks most frequently cause project delays in SAP ERP
   rollouts between 2022 and 2025?
3. Are there documented case studies where MES-SAP integration
   risk caused schedule overrun?
4. What mitigation best practices have been documented by
   SAP partner communities and PMI?

Structure: Per risk — source, industry context, and what
successful projects did differently.

What you get back: 15–20 industry-precedent risks, source-cited — which you can merge into the Perplexity output or use as a validation layer.

Step 3: Risk matrix and prioritization

With 20–30 risks in hand, ask Perplexity to prioritize and structure the steering committee view:

Based on my risk list below (paste your Step Perplexity output here):

1. Build a prioritized Top 10 risk list that I can present
   to the steering committee.

2. Group risks by urgency:
   - Requires immediate attention (red)
   - Monitor closely (yellow)
   - Accepted / low priority (green)

3. For each Top 10 risk, define an Early Warning Indicator:
   what should I watch for to detect this risk materializing
   before it becomes a crisis?

4. Write an executive summary (max 150 words) that I can
   drop into a steering committee presentation slide.

Format: Markdown with PowerPoint-compatible structure.

What you get back: A steering-committee-ready risk summary with early warning signals — in 3 minutes.

Step 4: Mitigation action plan

Take your Top 10 risks and generate a project-phased mitigation plan:

Based on my Top 10 risks, build a 90-day mitigation action plan.

Project context:
- Current phase: Blueprint (week 3)
- Go-live: 18 months from now
- Next milestone: Blueprint sign-off (week 6)

For each risk:
1. Recommended mitigation actions (concrete, measurable)
2. When should each be completed? (blueprint / realization / testing phase)
3. Who is responsible? (role)
4. How do we measure that mitigation is working?

Format: Gantt-compatible table that can be broken into Jira epics.

What you get back: A phase-scheduled mitigation plan with owners — ready to be loaded directly into Jira.

Step 5: Weekly refresh routine

A risk register is not a one-time artifact. Set up a 15-minute weekly Perplexity task:

My current risk register (paste document here):
[RISK REGISTER TEXT]

Last week's project events:
- [event 1, e.g., vendor delay on infrastructure delivery]
- [event 2, e.g., key SAP consultant on medical leave]
- [event 3, e.g., blueprint review received positive sponsor feedback]

Please:
1. Update risk scores based on this week's events
2. Flag which risks have become more acute and explain why
3. Identify any new risks that should be added
4. Write a 3-sentence weekly risk summary for the project sponsor

Format: Show only changes — diff format, not a full regeneration.

Time saved: 90 minutes per week down to 15 minutes.

📚 COOKBOOK — PRACTICAL RECIPES

Recipe #1: Rapid risk identification for an unfamiliar project type

The problem: You're running a project type you haven't led before (e.g., first cloud migration at your company) and have no personal experience base for risks.

Prompt:

I am an IT project manager leading a [PROJECT TYPE] project
at a [INDUSTRY] company for the first time.

Project parameters:
- Duration: [X months]
- Team size: [Y people]
- Technology stack: [list]
- Systems to integrate: [list]

Please identify 15 risks that an experienced PM would already
be accounting for in the initial planning phase.

Especially important: risks that remain hidden and typically
surface only in the middle of the project.

For each risk: description, probability, impact, and the
specific early signal that indicates it's developing.

How fast it works: 2–3 minutes

Result: 15 non-obvious risks that experienced PMs carry in institutional memory — now available to you on day one

Recipe #2: Stakeholder-specific risk communication

The problem: The same risk needs to be communicated differently to the CTO and the CFO.

Prompt:

The following risk exists in my project:
[RISK DESCRIPTION — e.g., "There is a 15–20% data loss risk during
migration if the legacy system is not adequately documented"]

Please frame this risk for three different audiences:

1. CTO (technical details, solution options, implementation impact)
2. CFO (financial impact, cost of inaction, budget requirements)
3. Business sponsor (project impact, decision required, timeline)

Each version should be max 5 sentences. Avoid technical jargon
in the CFO and sponsor versions.

How fast it works: 1–2 minutes

Result: Three communication-ready versions — no more "how do I explain this to leadership"

Recipe #3: Risk register import to Jira

The problem: The Perlpexity-generated risk register looks great, but moving it to Jira is manual work.

Prompt:

I have the following risk register in Markdown format:
[RISK REGISTER TEXT]

Convert this to CSV format compatible with Jira bulk import.
Columns should be:
- Summary (risk name)
- Description (risk description)
- Priority (High/Medium/Low — based on risk score)
- Labels (risk category)
- Assignee (responsible role — set to [PM Name] for now)
- Custom field: Risk Score (1–9)
- Custom field: Mitigation Status (Open)

Each risk should be a separate row.
Format: UTF-8 encoded CSV, compatible with Jira bulk import.

How fast it works: 1–2 minutes

Result: Jira-ready CSV file — 50 risks imported in minutes, not hours

Recipe #4: Risk register audit — reviewing a stale register

The problem: You have a risk register that was built 6 months ago and hasn't been properly maintained.

Prompt:

The following risk register was created 6 months ago
for [PROJECT NAME]:
[RISK REGISTER TEXT]

Current project status:
- Phase: [current phase]
- Time elapsed: [X months into the plan]
- Key changes over the past 6 months: [list]

Please:
1. Identify which risks are likely stale (no longer relevant)
2. Which risks have materialized and are now active issues,
   not just risks?
3. What new risks have likely emerged given the current phase
   and the changes you've listed?
4. Prioritize: what needs to be updated immediately?

Output: Edited risk register with change tracking annotations.

How fast it works: 3–5 minutes

Result: A current-state risk register audit that replaced 2 hours of manual review

💡 CLOSING THOUGHT

AI doesn't remove your accountability for risk management — that responsibility stays with you. What it removes is the 6 hours you would have spent on documentation instead of decision-making.

The best project managers don't identify the most risks. They identify the right ones, early enough to act. AI expands your peripheral vision: more perspectives, faster coverage, broader pattern recognition. The judgment, the prioritization, the stakeholder conversation — that's still yours.

Try the Step 1 prompt before your next risk kickoff workshop. The quality of that conversation changes completely when the team walks in with an AI-generated baseline to react to, rather than a blank whiteboard to fill.
